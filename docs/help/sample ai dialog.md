# Dialog with AI:

The dialog below was between a developer and CODEX, even though the app was generated by Claude:

This demonstrates how Architectural and Design decisions are traceable:

<p style="color:#35c735;">
>Prompt: question about th e Logical model why do I need audit entry as an entity
</pv>

You need AuditEntry as an entity when audit history is a business requirement, not just a technical log.

In a leave management system, it matters because you often must answer:

Who approved/rejected/cancelled a leave request
What changed (dates, status, reason, balance impact)
When it changed
Why it changed (comment, policy reason)
Without an audit entity, you only keep current state, and you lose traceability.

Use AuditEntry if you need:

Compliance and HR/legal defensibility
Dispute resolution
Forensic debugging and accountability
Reporting on workflow behavior
You can skip modeling it as a core domain entity if:

Audit is purely infrastructure-level (DB triggers/app logs)
Business users never query or report on it
No policy/regulatory requirement exists
Practical rule: if “history of change” is part of business truth, model AuditEntry explicitly in the logical model.


<p style="color:#35c735;">
>Prompt: so why in the logical model? What pattern motivated this design?
</p>
AI:

In the logical model, AuditEntry is there because the design follows a history-as-data pattern, not just state-as-data.

Pattern motivation:

Audit Trail pattern: treat change history as a first-class business record.
Temporal modeling: model “what was true at time T,” not only “what is true now.”
Event-style thinking (lightweight Event Sourcing influence): each meaningful state change is recorded as an immutable entry.
So the logical model includes it to represent a business capability: traceable decisions and changes.
If audit were only operational logging, it would stay in technical/infrastructure design, not logical domain design.

Commentary:
This answer is important because it separates business traceability from operational logging. The AI is not just defending a schema choice; it is identifying the business condition under which the entity belongs in the logical model.

<p style="color:#35c735;">
>Prompt: why are we not recorded the userId that, createdAt , updatedAt but not by whom
</p>

AI:
Good catch. createdAt/updatedAt without createdBy/updatedBy is incomplete for accountability.

Why it happens:

Teams add timestamps for technical concurrency/debugging first.
“By whom” is often deferred until audit/compliance needs become explicit.
Sometimes identity is assumed to be in external logs, which is weak for business traceability.
What to do in your logical model:

Add createdByUserId
Add updatedByUserId
For approval flows, also explicit action fields (for example approvedByUserId, rejectedByUserId) or keep them in AuditEntry events.
Rule of thumb:

If a human or service can change business state, record both when and who in domain data (or guaranteed domain audit records).

<p style="color:#35c735;">
>Prompt: where would add this rule to a skill or architecture concern?
</p>


Put it in both, but with different roles:

Architecture concern (source of truth)
Define it as a cross-cutting rule: “All business-state entities must capture createdBy/updatedBy (or equivalent actor trace).”
This belongs in your architecture principles/concerns because it is a system-wide design decision.

Skill (execution/enforcement)
Encode the same rule in the skill prompts/checklists/templates so generated models/docs/code always include it.
This belongs in the skill because it operationalizes the architecture rule during generation.

Short version: architecture concern defines the policy; skill enforces it consistently.
